# Reference Style Notes

Date: 2026-06-13
Reference images: user-provided Xiaohongshu deconstructed OOTD boards

## What To Borrow

- Vertical phone-friendly OOTD board.
- Central outfit figure with surrounding item breakdowns.
- Paper-texture or soft neutral background.
- Hand-drawn arrows and sketch-like annotation lines.
- Garment cutout zones: outerwear, top, bottom, shoes, bag, accessories.
- Fabric texture close-ups and color swatches.
- Editorial collage feeling instead of rigid UI cards.
- Short labels rather than long paragraphs.
- A semi-realistic illustrated figure can work better than a photorealistic model when the goal is advice rather than try-on.

## What Not To Copy

- Do not copy the exact layout, character, pose, or composition of the reference images.
- Do not use English pseudo-labels as the main text style; this skill should use Chinese.
- Do not include lingerie/underwear panels.
- Do not include nail close-ups or manicure content.
- Do not include makeup items, lipstick, compact powder, skincare, wallet contents, phones, notebooks, pens, receipts, cards, or unrelated lifestyle objects.
- Do not turn the board into a personal life inventory.

## Skill-Specific Direction

The board should stay useful for outfit decisions:

- clothes
- bag
- shoes
- accessories
- outerwear
- fabric/material
- color palette
- proportion/silhouette
- occasion fit
- risk reminder

Preferred style name inside the skill: `deconstructed OOTD board`.

Preferred figure style:

- semi-realistic fashion illustration
- natural adult proportions
- clean Korean/Japanese magazine illustration feel
- not photorealistic by default
- not anime, not childish, not celebrity-like
- outfit accuracy matters more than facial detail

Default visual hierarchy:

1. Central complete outfit.
2. Around it: top, bottom, shoes, bag, outerwear, accessories.
3. Optional: fabric swatch and color palette.
4. Minimal Chinese labels and arrows.

This style should feel warm, editorial, and hand-annotated, but remain clean and practical.

## Realistic vs Illustrated Figure

Photorealistic model:

- Pros: stronger real-world material feeling, easier to imagine final look.
- Cons: can imply virtual try-on, over-focus on face/body, feel like an ad, and create false precision about fit.

Illustrated figure:

- Pros: better matches deconstructed OOTD collage, feels advisory, reduces identity/body pressure, and makes the board more portable across users.
- Cons: can become too cute or anime if not constrained; may weaken fabric realism.

Decision:

- Default to semi-realistic fashion illustration.
- Use photorealistic editorial style only when requested.
- Keep real garment/detail cutouts realistic enough to show fabric, color, and silhouette.
